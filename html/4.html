Opinion: Justice is served as 18C heroes are finally exonerated

Rowan Dean, The Courier-Mail
November 7, 2016 1:00am
Subscriber only

MY NOMINATION for 2017 Queenslander of the Year: Alex Wood. Runners-up: Jackson Powell and Calum Thwaites.

These three heroes deserve to be household names the length and breadth of the Sunshine State, and across the nation.

On Friday afternoon, they won the most important battle of their, and every freedom-loving Australian’s, lives.

Thanks to the ruling by Federal Court Circuit judge Michael Jarrett, they are now free of being prosecuted under 18C for the vile and false accusation of “racial vilification”.

But no thanks whatsoever to Gillian Triggs and Tim Soutphommasane of the all-powerful, ideologically obsessed Australian Human Rights Commission.

For the past 3½ years, Wood, Powell and Thwaites have lived with a heavy, dark and sinister cloud hovering over their heads, careers, reputations and wallets.

Most of us are familiar with the bare bones of their nightmarish ordeal – which could happen to any of us, by the way – of being accused under section 18C of the Racial Discrimination Act for offending someone without even realising we’d done it.

But just to reiterate: in May 2013, three white students wandered into a Queensland University of Technology computer lab which had been set aside – outrageously, in my opinion – for indigenous students only.

Nobody had bothered to put up a sign to tell anyone of this stupidity.

An Aboriginal administrator, Cindy Prior, told the boys to leave, which they did. Later, they complained on Facebook that this was “segregation”. Which it was, and is. Subsequent online comments included a gag about “where’s the white supremacist computer lab?”.

The judge said this was a “poor attempt at humour” – the only thing in his ruling I disagree with. It was a perfectly acceptable joke, given the scenario. And I get nervous when the law starts proscribing how good a joke is or isn’t.

Anyway, Ms Prior chose to feel threatened and offended by these comments and jokes – even though they weren’t addressed at her – and, encouraged by Triggs’ secretive Human Rights Commission, embarked on a legal attempt to sue seven students for $250,000.

This is a vast sum of money for a normal person, but if you’re a sanctimonious Human Rights president earning nearly half a million dollars a year, I guess it’s not that much.

Some of the kids coughed up five grand each for Prior to go away.

Wood, Powell and Thwaites bravely decided to fight this vile injustice.

On Friday, Judge Jarrett declared them completely innocent with no case to answer.

These three are national heroes. They fought for the freedoms of every one of us.

Suburbs and roads should be named in their honour, and every Aussie schoolkid taught of their bravery.

If you see them, shake their hands. If you have a baby, name him Alex, Jackson or Calum. “As brave as the 18C boys” should enter the vernacular. That would go a tiny way towards making up for the terrible turmoil they and their families suffered.

Yet Cindy Prior is also a victim – not of the seven white boys, but of the entire Aboriginal Grievance Industry, which is now blighting so many indigenous lives.

This is an industry made up of left-wing, ideological activists and politicians desperate to make indigenous Australians feel they are being threatened and oppressed by “white privilege” and “racism”.

This has led to the disgusting situation where lawyers – and the Race Discrimination Commissioner – are actively encouraging black Australians to feel offended, insulted or in some way aggrieved by white Australians, and to seek financial redress.

As The Australian newspaper reported, not only did Soutphommasane urge Aboriginal people to complain about Bill Leak’s “racist” cartoon, but two white lawyers wandered around the outback looking for Aborigines to also sign complaint forms about it.

This is a national disgrace.

Leak and his family are now going through the same 18C nightmare the three boys did.

Calum Thwaites was so traumatised by his ordeal, he abandoned his chosen career of teaching disadvantaged indigenous youth.

Those kids he would have taught and helped are also collateral damage of 18C.

Triggs, Soutphommasane, and the entire Aboriginal Grievance Industry stand condemned over these travesties of justice.

Yet one Queensland man could, if he were as brave as Wood, Powell and Thwaites, return sanity to our justice system: Federal Attorney-General George Brandis.

Section 18C must be amended and a new president appointed to clean out the HRC cesspit.

Do it now George, and you’d be a hero too.
